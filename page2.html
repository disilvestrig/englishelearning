TO INFINITIVE

Present Past Progressive form
to be/not to be to have been/not to have been //
to leave/not to leave to have left/not to have left to be leaving/to have been leaving
to work/not to work to have worked/not to have worked to be working/to have been working
  • We can build the negative form by putting not before the infinitive. The infinitive can't be preceded by a preposition (ex. : They told us not to take photographs).
  • After verbs like go, come, run, hurry up, stay, stop,the infinitive is replaced by and in imperative forms (ex. : Come and help me with these shopping bags).
  • And can be used instead of the infinitive also with other tenses, but not with progressive forms (ex. : We're out of milk. I'll go and get some).

USES OF THE INFINITIVE

  • As the subject of a sentence, but it is more common to use the impersonal subject it or the form in -ing (ex. : To live alone requires great courage).
  • After many adjectives, verbs and some nouns (ex.: I'm pleased to see everybody is having a good time)
  • In place of a relative sentence (ex. : We weren't the only ones to complain).
  • After how, who, what, when, where, whether (ex.: Show me how to operate this machine).
  • The to of the infinitive can be omitted after help ( ex. : James helped us (to) redecorate our living room) .
  • And also in front of the second infinitive in coordinated propositions (ex. : Do you prefer to stay here and rest, or go out ? ).
  • It is always omitted after why or why not used at the beginning of a sentence to give a hint (ex.: Why phone him? Why not send him a text?).

Want, would like/love/prefer

  • Want and the conditional forms would like/love/prefer are usually followed by infinity. When you specify who you want to do something, they are followed by an object + infinite complement, never that (eg: Do you want to leave?).

Infinitive of purpose or for + -ing

  • The purpose is usually expressed with a simple infinitive. You can use for + -ing to specify the general function of an object, the purpose it serves. To questions like What's it used for? you can therefore answer in two ways, depending on what you mean. However, the infinitive must be used when referring to a specific action (es. : Tim moved to Edinburgh to live closer to his girlfriend

Implied infinitive

  • An infinitive can only be represented by the particle to to avoid repeating a previously expressed verb. This usually occurs after verbs that express will or intention such as want, wish, love, like, prefer, hope, try, mean, intend ... and with the forms have to, need to, ought to, be able to, used to, be going to ... (ex. : Jason suggested ordering a Chinese takeaway, but I don't want to. ).

Past or progressive infinity

  • Infinity can be used in the past and progressive forms (ex.: I was glad to have missed that awful scene.).
